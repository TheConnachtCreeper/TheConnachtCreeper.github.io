Púcaí

Have you ever wanted a pet that could change shape on a whim, predict the future, and control an entire harvest? Well, then have I got a creature for you! Of course, the púca (spelled pooka, in English) of Ireland is definitely not a pet, but it is a fascinating mythical creature that can do all the things I just listed and more.

While some accounts lump púcaí (or pookas) in with fairy creatures, I am of the firm opinion that púcaí are not fairies. Most accounts of púcaí specifically say that they are animal shape shifters (and can take such shapes as goat, dog, rabbit, donkey, horse, etc.), while fairies are very specifically human in appearance.

The origins of the word púca are unclear, although some say that it is derived from an ancient word for “goat” (and this is occasionally used to explain that the púca is a form of the devil). There are also various different interpretations on the meaning of the word púca: modern accounts seem to be split evenly between “ghost” and “goblin”.

Whichever versions of the etymology and meaning of the word you choose to believe, there are many, many stories surrounding the púca. One of the most well-known stories is of the púca in horse form. It is said that the púca likes to frequent rural country areas where it can assume its horse form and gallop through fields, hedges, and fences, occasionally leaving destruction in its wake.

When it is feeling mischievous, the púca in horse form will throw a human onto its back and take them for a terror-inducing gallop through the countryside. This most typically happens to drunkards leaving the pub at night after last-call; a large black horse with glowing orange eyes will race with the drunkard on its back throughout the night and at sunrise deposit them in the middle of a field with little to no recollection of how they got there.

Other stories surrounding the púca deal with the highly important harvest rituals in October and November. The púca is said to spoil any crops that are left in the fields after October 31. This corresponds to the official change from autumn to winter in the ancient Celtic calendar, which is marked by November 1 and the festival of Samhain.

The month of November is said to be the púca’s month, and if there is any time that púcaí are charitable toward humans, it is then. But Halloween night (Oiche Samhain) is when the púca is up to the most mischief, and only the very daring will go out to walk with púca.

While the púca can take any form, it is known to have the gift of human speech in all its forms. Indeed, sometimes the púca appears as a human! As with all other stories surrounding the púca, there are a myriad of accounts regarding the púca in human form. In some cases, it is as simple as an old man who sits next to you and talks your ear off as you rest alone on a bench.

In other cases, the púca will approach you directly, as a stranger, and proceed to tell you things about yourself and your family that only you would know - sometimes ending the soliloquy with predictions of the future which end up coming true! People who believe the púca exists in Ireland to this day are most likely to point to interactions like this which happened to them or an acquaintance as proof.
